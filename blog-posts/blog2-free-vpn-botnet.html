The Business Model: How Free VPNs Actually Make Money

Running a VPN is not free. Servers cost money. Bandwidth costs money. Engineering costs money. When you use a VPN that charges you nothing, that cost is being covered somehow. Understanding the possible revenue models tells you how much risk you're carrying.

The legitimate model: A small number of providers offer free tiers funded by their paid subscriber base. Proton VPN, Windscribe, and PrivadoVPN operate this way. The free tier is a funnel for paid conversion. These providers have financial incentive to maintain a good reputation — which means not doing anything terrible to free users.

The data harvesting model: Many free VPNs log and sell user data — browsing history, DNS queries, connection metadata — to advertising networks and data brokers. A Top10VPN investigation of 100 free Android VPN apps found that nearly 90% leaked some form of user data. This is invasive but not the worst outcome.

The bandwidth harvesting model: The most dangerous category. These VPNs install a background service that routes other people's traffic through your device and internet connection. Your IP address is enrolled in a commercial proxy network. Criminals and advertisers pay for access to it. You become an unwitting proxy node — and your IP gets flagged for anything those users do through it.

The pure malware model: The 911 S5 case. The app is VPN software in name only. Its actual function is to install a persistent backdoor that enrolls your device in a criminal botnet. Your bandwidth and IP are sold to cybercriminals specifically. This is the extreme outcome — and the FBI has documented that it reached 19 million devices.

The 911 S5 Botnet: 19 Million Hijacked Devices

In May 2024, the FBI — working with law enforcement partners across multiple countries — dismantled what investigators described as the largest botnet ever discovered at that time. The operation was called "Operation Tunnel Rat."

The network was called 911 S5. It had been operating since 2014, built steadily across a decade by distributing free VPN applications. By the time of its dismantlement, it comprised over 19 million unique IP addresses spanning more than 190 countries.

The free VPN apps used to build the botnet included: MaskVPN, DewVPN, PaladinVPN, ProxyGate, ShieldVPN, and ShineVPN — plus at least 12 additional apps identified in the investigation. Every user who installed these apps had their device silently converted into a proxy server.

Cybercriminals paid the botnet operators for access to specific residential IPs. They used those IPs to commit fraud that appeared to come from legitimate user addresses: pandemic relief fraud, unemployment benefit fraud, credit card fraud, and cyberattacks that were routed through the hijacked devices to conceal the attackers' real locations.

The botnet earned its operators an estimated $99 million. Losses to victims — in confirmed cases alone — exceeded $5.9 billion, with a substantial portion traced to fraudulent claims against US government pandemic assistance programs.

The alleged ringleader, Yunhe Wang, was arrested in Singapore on May 24, 2024. But the apps had been circulating since 2011. The malware ran silently on millions of devices for over a decade. Even after the FBI seizure of the botnet's infrastructure, devices with the malware installed remained compromised until the software was manually removed.

How Free VPN Hijacking Works Technically

The mechanism varies by sophistication level, but the core approach is consistent across known cases.

Installation phase: The user downloads what appears to be a legitimate VPN app — complete with app store listing, ratings, screenshots, and privacy policy. The app may genuinely function as a VPN for basic traffic. The malicious component runs in parallel.

Background service installation: The app installs a background service — often disguised as a maintenance process with names like "MaskVPN.exe" or a system-sounding process name. This service persists after the VPN app is closed, survives restarts, and in some cases reinstalls itself if removed.

Proxy enrollment: The background service connects to command-and-control servers and registers the device as an available proxy node. It reports the device's IP address, connection speed, and geographic location. The device is then added to the commercial proxy pool available to paying customers.

Traffic routing: When a criminal pays for access to a residential IP in your city, their traffic is routed through your device. Your internet connection carries their requests. Your IP address appears in server logs at the target site — not theirs. If they commit fraud, the evidence points to you.

SDK-level infection: More recent variants use monetization SDKs embedded in the app rather than separate malware. In a 2024 investigation, security researchers found dozens of apps using a service called LumiApps, which ostensibly provided monetization for app developers but silently enrolled users' devices in a proxy network. The apps themselves passed automated malware scans because the proxy SDK wasn't technically malware — it was a "feature."

The Hola VPN model (legal but exploitative): Hola VPN has operated this way openly since at least 2015. It sells users' bandwidth through its commercial service, Luminati (now rebranded as Bright Data). Hola is upfront about this in its terms of service — which most users never read. The bandwidth harvesting is disclosed. This is legally different from the 911 S5 model but functionally similar from the user's perspective.

🔍 Critical Point on Hola VPN

Hola VPN and Bright Data are related — Hola's free users fund the residential proxy network. This is disclosed in Hola's terms. Bright Data itself is a legitimate commercial proxy business. The problem is that Hola users rarely understand they're contributing their IP to a commercial service. If you're using Hola for "privacy," you don't have any.

Red Flags: How to Spot a Malicious Free VPN

The 911 S5 botnet apps looked indistinguishable from legitimate VPN apps in the Play Store. They had ratings, reviews, and reasonable download counts. App store presentation is not a safety signal. Here's what actually matters:

No paid tier and no stated funding model. If the VPN is entirely free and the company has no premium offering, no stated enterprise service, and no disclosed funding source, your data or bandwidth is the product. Full stop.

"Super", "Turbo", "Ultra", "Shield", or "Mask" in the name. This naming pattern is consistent across documented malicious VPN apps. It's not a guarantee of danger, but it's a reliable red flag that warrants extra scrutiny.

No published security audit. Legitimate privacy-focused VPN providers publish independent security audits from firms like Cure53, SEC Consult, or Trail of Bits. If a VPN can't point to a published audit, there's no evidence it does what it claims.

Excessive permissions. A VPN app needs network access. It doesn't need access to your contacts, camera, microphone, storage, or location in the background. Permissions beyond network access are a strong signal of secondary functionality.

Unknown parent company. Many free VPN apps are owned by holding companies that operate dozens of brands simultaneously. The CISA December 2024 mobile security guidance specifically cited opaque ownership as a risk factor. If you can't verify who owns the VPN, assume the worst.

High star ratings with low review quality. Research projects that up to 37% of app store reviews could be fake by 2025. The 911 S5 botnet apps had acceptable ratings. Review count is not a safety indicator.

Data Harvesting: The Quieter Threat Most Free VPNs Run

Below the botnet level, there's a much more common problem: data logging and sale. Most free VPNs that aren't outright malicious are still harvesting and monetizing your data.

A 2024 Top10VPN investigation of 100 free Android VPN apps found that nearly 90% leaked some form of user data. More than a third used encryption weaker than current standards despite claiming to protect user traffic. Nearly 70% requested permissions with no legitimate VPN use case.

A separate Zimperium zLabs investigation of over 800 free VPN apps found hundreds that offered no real encryption at all — despite advertising themselves as privacy tools. Traffic the user believed was encrypted was traveling in plain text.

The categories of data typically harvested and sold:

DNS query logs — Every domain name you look up tells a story about your interests, health concerns, financial situation, and behavior. DNS logs are extremely valuable to data brokers and advertisers.

Connection metadata — Which servers you connect to, when, for how long, and from what location. Metadata patterns reveal behavior even without content inspection.

Device identifiers — Advertising IDs, device fingerprints, and hardware identifiers allow persistent tracking across sessions even when you change IPs.

Unencrypted traffic interception — Some free VPN apps perform SSL interception, replacing legitimate certificates with their own to read HTTPS traffic. This is the most dangerous form — and it's documented in real apps.

When You Need More Than a Free VPN

For personal privacy on untrusted networks, the three providers above cover most cases. But for business use — web scraping, ad verification, geo-testing, market research, or bypassing sophisticated bot detection — a VPN isn't the right tool regardless of whether it's free or paid.

Business use cases require residential proxy networks: pools of real consumer IPs with genuine ISP assignments, geographic targeting, and rotation capabilities that no VPN can match. The key difference from the malicious free VPN model: legitimate proxy providers compensate device owners who voluntarily opt their devices into the network and disclose exactly how the network is built.

FAQ: Free VPN Botnet Risk

Can a free VPN turn my device into a botnet node?

Yes — and it's documented. The FBI dismantled the 911 S5 botnet in May 2024, built through free VPN apps that converted 19 million devices into proxy nodes. The apps were on Google Play, had ratings, and functioned as VPNs while running a silent background service.

How do free VPNs steal your bandwidth?

They install a background service that routes third-party traffic through your internet connection. Your IP is enrolled in a commercial proxy pool. People pay to use your connection — you carry their traffic without knowing it.

Which free VPN apps are dangerous in 2026?

FBI-documented: MaskVPN, DewVPN, PaladinVPN, ProxyGate, ShieldVPN, ShineVPN. High-risk by pattern: any free VPN with no paid tier, no published audit, or "Super/Turbo/Ultra/Shield/Mask" in the name. Hola VPN openly sells your bandwidth (disclosed in ToS but widely misunderstood).

What's the difference between malicious free VPN proxy networks and legitimate residential proxy providers?

Consent and transparency. Legitimate providers (Bright Data, Oxylabs, IPRoyal's peer network) recruit device owners who knowingly opt in and receive compensation. Malicious free VPNs enroll devices covertly, without disclosure, without consent, and without compensation.

What should I use instead of a free VPN?

For personal privacy: Proton VPN Free (unlimited data, audited, Swiss jurisdiction). For business use cases requiring residential IPs: Bright Data, Oxylabs, or Decodo — all transparent about network sourcing and audited for security.

Don't Let a "Free" VPN Own Your Connection

If you need residential IPs for business — scraping, verification, geo-testing — use a provider that's transparent about its network. If you need personal privacy, use an audited VPN with a real business model.
